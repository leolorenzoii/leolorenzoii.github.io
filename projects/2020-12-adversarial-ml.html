Adversarial Machine Learning

December 2020

Introduction

In the past decade, deep neural networks created machine learning algorithms that exceed human capability (see Figure below). Deep learning algorithms are indeed powerful and can be seen applied in our everyday lives extensively. However, it was found that deep learning algorithms suffer a fatal flaw, they can be easily fooled through the use of adversarial examples.

Here, the same gradient descent algorithm we use in optimizing our network parameters, is repurposed to fool the network. Defenses to these attacks include adversarial training, purification or distillation of adversarial examples, and randomization. These defenses mainly propose modifying the input sample to treat adversarial examples and are usually computationally expensive.

In this project, we propose to look at a different lens by looking at the loss landscape created by the activation functions. Specifically, we answer the problem “What is the role of activation functions in adversarial robustness?”. Our main motivation is to help deep learning practitioners to be aware and wisely choose their activation functions, if in the case were adversarial defenses are not readily available or possible at their setting.

Methodology

We create train and test simulations of different scenarios to investigate the role of activation functions in adversarial robustness. We use the MNIST dataset, which contains 60,000 training and 10,000 test images of handwritten digits, and the CIFAR-10 dataset, which contains 60,000 32x32 colour images with 10 classes and 6000 images per class. We used 8 activation functions in our simulations namely, elu, gelu, mish, swish, relu, tanh, selu, and sigmoid. Then trained using two scenarios, standard and adversarial. Finally, we tested the models using clean and adversarially generated samples.

Results

Three key results were found from our simulation. First, our results echo the proposition that adversarial robustness has an inherent trade-off with accuracy. Second, the choice of the optimal activation functions is not only problem dependent but training dependent. Finally, we found that more complicated functions provide consistent accuracy and robustness performance as compared to traditional activation functions relu and tanh.

For the data science practitioners and enthusiasts, our result show the importance of exploring the correct activation functions for the problem at hand. Indeed, factors such as robustness, security, efficiency, and accuracy play a role in choosing the right architecture and choice of activation functions.

Recommendations

Here are some possible extensions of this study:

  1. Understand on a deeper level why some activation functions are more preferred than others.
  2. Craft scalable adversarial defenses using the knowledge that activation functions play a vital role in robustness.
  3. Extend the methodology to other problems or datasets.
